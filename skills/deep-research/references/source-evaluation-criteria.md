# Source Evaluation Criteria

A systematic framework for evaluating the reliability, relevance, and
trustworthiness of information sources used in research. Apply these criteria
consistently to ensure defensible conclusions.

---

## Source Tiering System

All sources fall into one of three tiers. Higher tiers carry more weight in
evidence-based conclusions, but lower-tier sources still have valid uses
(background context, leads to primary sources, sentiment indicators).

---

### Tier 1: Primary Sources

Sources that present original data, firsthand accounts, or original research.
These are the strongest evidence for factual claims.

| Source Type | Description | Examples |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Peer-reviewed research | Original studies published in academic journals with formal peer review | Papers in Nature, NEJM, ACL proceedings |
| Official company filings | Legally mandated disclosures | SEC 10-K/10-Q, annual reports, prospectuses |
| Government data | Direct statistical collections or regulatory filings | Census Bureau data, BLS statistics, FDA approvals |
| Firsthand accounts | Direct testimony from participants or witnesses | Interview transcripts, oral histories, autobiographies |
| Original datasets | Raw data collected by the researcher or organization | Survey datasets, experimental results, sensor data |
| Patent filings | Original technical disclosures | USPTO, EPO, WIPO patent applications |
| Legal documents | Court filings, legislation, regulatory text | Court opinions, statutes, agency rules |
| Internal company documents | Leaked or disclosed internal communications | Memo, strategy docs, emails (verify authenticity) |

**When to prefer Tier 1:** Supporting factual claims about what happened, what was
measured, what was said, or what was decided. Any claim about performance,
outcomes, or specific events.

**Caveat:** Primary sources are not automatically trustworthy. A peer-reviewed
paper with a flawed methodology is primary but unreliable. A company's own
marketing claims in an annual report are primary but self-serving. Always apply
the reliability scoring rubric (below) even to Tier 1 sources.

---

### Tier 2: Secondary Sources

Sources that analyze, interpret, summarize, or comment on primary sources.
They add context but introduce interpretation risk.

| Source Type | Description | Examples |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Analyst reports | Professional analysis of markets, companies, or technologies | Gartner, Forrester, IDC, CB Insights |
| Review articles | Academic papers that synthesize multiple primary studies | Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, literature reviews |
| Investigative journalism | In-depth reporting based on multiple primary sources | NYT investigation, ProPublica, Bloomberg long-form |
 Industry white papers | Technical or market analyses published by companies | AWS architecture guides, McKinsey insights |
 | Textbooks | Synthesized knowledge from a field | University press textbooks, O'Reilly books |
 | Expert commentary | Analysis by recognized domain experts | Op-eds by practitioners, expert blog posts |
 | Documentary evidence | Films, podcasts, or multimedia based on research | Documentaries with cited sources, investigative podcasts |

**When to prefer Tier 2:** Understanding context, getting expert interpretation,
comparing multiple primary sources, identifying trends across studies.

**Caveat:** Secondary sources filter and interpret primary data. The analyst's
biases, methodology, and conflicts of interest matter enormously. A Forrester
report commissioned by a vendor is Tier 2 but should be treated with caution.
Always trace the analysis back to its primary sources when possible.

---

### Tier 3: Tertiary Sources

Sources that compile or aggregate information from primary and secondary sources.
Good for orientation and broad context, weak for specific claims.

| Source Type | Description | Examples |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Encyclopedias | General knowledge summaries | Wikipedia, Britannica |
 | News aggregators | Compilations of reporting from other outlets | Google News, Techmeme, Apple News |
 | Social media posts | Claims made without editorial oversight | Twitter/X threads, Reddit posts, LinkedIn posts |
 | Press releases | Self-published announcements | Company PR wires, product announcements |
 | Blog posts (non-expert) | Personal opinions without deep research | Personal blogs, Medium posts, Substack newsletters |
 | Forum discussions | Community opinions and Q&A | Stack Overflow, Quora, Hacker News threads |
 | AI-generated summaries | Content produced by language models | ChatGPT outputs, AI-written articles |

**When to use Tier 3:** Getting initial orientation on an unfamiliar topic,
finding leads to better sources, gauging public sentiment or community opinion,
brainstorming search terms.

**Caveat:** Tier 3 sources should almost never be cited as evidence for a
factual claim. Use them as starting points, not endpoints. Always verify
Tier 3 claims against Tier 1 or Tier 2 sources before including them in
conclusions.

---

## Reliability Scoring Rubric

Score each source on five dimensions, each worth 0-2 points, for a total
of 0-10. This score determines how much weight to give the source in your
analysis.

---

### Scoring Dimensions

#### 1. Authority (0-2 points)

Who created this source, and what qualifies them to speak on this topic?

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|----------|
| 0 | Author/creator unknown or clearly unqualified. No credentials, institutional affiliation, or track record. |
| 1 | Author has some relevant qualification (degree, experience, affiliation) but it's not their primary expertise. Or: well-known institution but the specific author is unknown. |
| 2 | Author is a recognized expert with deep domain expertise. They have a track record of reliable work on this specific topic. Institutional backing enhances credibility. |

**Check:** Does the author have advanced degrees, relevant job titles, prior
publications, or professional recognition in this field? Have they been cited
by other experts? Do they have a financial or ideological stake in the claims?

#### 2. Accuracy (0-2 points)

Is the information correct, and can claims be verified against other sources?

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|----------|
| 0 | Multiple factual errors detected. Claims cannot be verified. No sources cited for factual assertions. |
| 1 | Mostly accurate but some claims are unverifiable or minor errors exist. Some sourcing provided. |
| 2 | All verifiable claims check out. Primary sources are cited. Methodology is transparent. Findings are reproducible. |

**Check:** Cross-reference key claims against at least two independent sources.
Look for specific numbers, dates, and proper nouns that can be verified. Check
whether the source corrects errors when identified.

#### 3. Currency (0-2 points)

How recent is the information, and is it current enough for the topic?

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|----------|
| 0 | Information is significantly outdated for the topic. No indication of when it was last updated. |
| 1 | Information is somewhat dated but core findings may still be relevant. The source acknowledges its publication date. |
| 2 | Information is recent (see date relevance guidelines below) or the topic is sufficiently timeless that recency is less critical. |

**Check:** When was this published? When was the underlying data collected? Has
the landscape changed since publication? Does the source provide updates?

#### 4. Objectivity (0-2 points)

Is the source presented fairly, or does it show clear bias?

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|----------|
| 0 | Clear bias, conflict of interest, or persuasive intent. Evidence is cherry-picked. Opposing views are ignored or misrepresented. |
| 1 | Minor bias present (tone, framing) but overall evidence is presented with reasonable balance. Sponsorship or affiliation is disclosed. |
| 2 | Neutral tone. Multiple perspectives presented. Methodology designed to minimize bias. Conflicts of interest disclosed and managed. |

**Check:** Who funded this? Is the author advocating for a specific position?
Are counterarguments addressed? Is language loaded or neutral? Does the source
profit from you believing its claims?

#### 5. Coverage (0-2 points)

How thoroughly does the source address the question at hand?

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|----------|
| 0 | Superficial treatment. Important aspects of the topic are missing. The source barely addresses the relevant question. |
| 1 | Adequate coverage of the main aspects but lacks depth on nuances or edge cases. Some relevant subtopics are missing. |
| 2 | Comprehensive treatment. Multiple facets explored. Methodology covers important variables. Limitations are acknowledged. |

**Check:** Does the source address the specific question you're researching, or
only an adjacent topic? Does it go deep enough for your needs? Are important
contextual factors considered?

---

### Interpreting the Total Score

| Score Range | Rating | How to Use |
|:-----------:|--------|-----------|
| 9-10 | Highly Reliable | Can serve as a key evidence source. Cite with high confidence. |
| 7-8 | Reliable | Solid source for most claims. Minor limitations noted in caveats. |
| 5-6 | Moderately Reliable | Useful but should be corroborated with at least one other source. |
| 3-4 | Low Reliability | Use only for background context. Do not rely on for specific claims. |
| 0-2 | Unreliable | Avoid. If you must reference, clearly label as unreliable and triangulate. |

**Important:** The score is a heuristic, not a mathematical formula. Use your
judgment. A source that scores 6 but is a primary dataset directly relevant to
your question may be more valuable than a source that scores 8 but only
tangentially addresses the topic.

## Domain-Specific Trust Indicators

Different domains have different signals of trustworthiness. Use these
checklists alongside the general reliability rubric.

---

### Academic Sources

**Trust indicators:**
- Published in a journal with a reputable peer-review process
- Journal has a reasonable impact factor or is well-regarded in its field
- Authors are affiliated with recognized research institutions
- Study design is appropriate for the research question
- Sample size is adequate and methodology is transparent
- Findings have been replicated or supported by subsequent research
- Declaration of interest / funding disclosure is included
- Pre-registered (for clinical trials and some social science research)

**Red flags:**
- Published in a predatory journal (no real peer review, exists to collect fees)
- Author list includes unqualified or honorary authors
- No ethical approval mentioned for human/animal studies
- Statistical errors or p-hacking indicators
- Results seem too good to be true (massive effect sizes, near-perfect accuracy)

---

### Industry / Corporate Sources

**Trust indicators:**
- Claims are backed by specific, verifiable data
- Third-party validation (certifications, audits, benchmark results)
- Published by established companies with reputations to protect
- Methodology is described (how metrics were calculated, what was measured)
- Comparisons are fair (apples-to-apples, not favorable cherry-picking)
- Limitations are acknowledged

**Red flags:**
- Vague superlatives without evidence ("industry-leading," "best-in-class")
- Only favorable metrics shown; unfavorable ones omitted
- No methodology description
- Comparisons against outdated competitor products or straw-man alternatives
- Claims that contradict independent assessments
- Published by a company with a direct financial interest in the conclusion

---

### Government Sources

**Trust indicators:**
- Data comes from the official statistical agency (Census, BLS, BEA, etc.)
- Methodology documentation is available and transparent
- Data is collected through rigorous, standardized processes
- Historical data is available for trend analysis
- Independent analysts have validated the data
- Source is non-partisan (statistical agencies vs. political offices)

**Red flags:**
- Data from political appointees' offices rather than career staff agencies
- Methodology changes that coincidentally produce favorable numbers
- Data that has been suppressed, delayed, or altered for political reasons
- No documentation of methodology or sampling approach
- Outdated data presented as current

---

### Media / Journalism Sources

**Trust indicators:**
- Published by a newsroom with editorial standards and fact-checking
- Multiple named sources quoted on the record
- Original reporting (not just republishing press releases or other outlets)
- Corrections policy is visible and used
- Reporter has a track record on the beat
- The piece distinguishes between reported facts and analysis/opinion

**Red flags:**
- Anonymous sourcing without editorial justification
- Headlines that don't match the article content
- No original reporting — entirely based on press releases or other outlets
- Strong editorializing in news (not opinion) sections
- Publisher known for partisan framing or clickbait
- No correction when factual errors are pointed out

---

### Community / Open-Source Sources

**Trust indicators:**
- Active maintainer(s) with history of quality contributions
- Multiple independent contributors (not a one-person project)
- Regular updates and responsiveness to issues/PRs
- Test coverage, CI/CD, and code review practices
- Positive signal from downstream users and dependent projects
- Documentation is thorough and maintained

**Red flags:**
- Single maintainer with no backup (bus factor = 1)
- No commits in 6+ months with open issues accumulating
- No tests, no code review, no CI
- Claims that seem exaggerated compared to actual usage metrics
- License ambiguity or IP concerns
- Known security vulnerabilities left unpatched

---

## Red Flags for Unreliable Sources

Watch for these warning signs regardless of source tier or domain:

### Content Red Flags
- **Extraordinary claims without extraordinary evidence:** Large performance
  numbers, breakthrough claims, or paradigm-shifting assertions with weak sourcing.
- **Cherry-picked data:** Only favorable data points shown; unfavorable ones
  omitted or explained away.
- **Circular sourcing:** Source A cites Source B, which cites Source A, creating
  the illusion of independent corroboration.
- **Vague attribution:** "Studies show," "experts say," "research indicates"
  without naming specific studies or experts.
- **Emotional manipulation:** Fear-mongering, urgency tactics, or appeals to
  identity rather than evidence.
- **Confirmation bias:** The source only presents evidence supporting one
  conclusion and ignores or dismisses counterevidence.

### Structural Red Flags
- **No publication date:** You cannot assess currency.
- **No author attribution:** You cannot assess authority or check track record.
- **No methodology description:** You cannot assess accuracy or reproduce findings.
- **No corrections policy:** Errors, once published, stand uncorrected.
- **Website design issues:** Numerous typos, broken links, missing About page,
  no contact information. (Not always indicative — but often correlated with
  low-quality sources.)
- **Domain spoofing:** URL designed to look like a reputable source
  (e.g., "bbcnews.com.co" instead of "bbc.com").

### Financial Red Flags
- **Undisclosed sponsorship:** The source is funded by a party with a direct
  interest in the conclusion, but this is not disclosed.
- **Pay-to-publish:** The source is effectively an advertisement dressed up as
  research or analysis.
- **Affiliate links or referral incentives:** The source profits from the
  reader taking a specific action (buying a product, signing up for a service).

## Handling Conflicting Sources

Conflicting information is normal in research. Here's how to handle it
systematically:

### Step 1: Verify the Conflict is Real
- Make sure the sources are actually talking about the same thing. Conflicts
  often arise from different definitions, scopes, or methodologies.
- Check if one source is more current — the landscape may have genuinely changed.

### Step 2: Assess Source Quality
- Apply the reliability scoring rubric to each conflicting source.
- Higher-scoring sources generally deserve more weight.
- If scores are similar, look at methodology transparency and sample size.

### Step 3: Examine Methodology Differences
- Are the sources measuring the same thing in the same way?
- Different methodologies (survey vs. transactional data, self-reported vs.
  observed, different sample frames) can legitimately produce different results.
- The "better" methodology depends on the question.

### Step 4: Look for Convergence Elsewhere
- Are there other sources that agree with Source A, Source B, or neither?
- A finding supported by multiple independent sources is stronger than a single
  source, even if that single source is high-quality.

### Step 5: Present the Conflict Honestly
- In your output, acknowledge the conflict explicitly.
- Explain why you weighted one source over the other (or why you couldn't).
- If the conflict is unresolvable, note it as an area of uncertainty.

**Template for documenting conflicts:**

```
Conflict: [Source A] states [claim X] while [Source B] states [claim Y].

Source A quality: [score] — [key strengths and weaknesses]
Source B quality: [score] — [key strengths and weaknesses]

Methodology comparison:
- Source A: [methodology, sample, date]
- Source B: [methodology, sample, date]

Resolution: [Explain which source you rely on and why, or note that the
conflict remains unresolved]

Confidence in resolution: [High / Medium / Low]
```

## Date Relevance Guidelines

How recent does a source need to be? It depends entirely on the topic. Use
these guidelines to assess whether data is too old for your needs.

---

### Fast-Moving Domains (12-18 month half-life)

In these domains, information older than 12-18 months should be treated with
caution. Information older than 3 years is likely outdated.

| Domain | Examples | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| AI / Machine Learning | Model benchmarks, framework capabilities, tooling | The field moves extremely fast. A 2022 benchmark may be irrelevant in 2024. |
| Cryptocurrency / Web3 | Market data, protocol specifications, regulatory status | Regulatory landscape changes rapidly. |
| Cybersecurity | Vulnerability data, threat landscapes, tool effectiveness | New vulnerabilities emerge constantly. |
| Social media / Platforms | Usage statistics, algorithm behavior, API capabilities | Platforms change features and policies frequently. |
| Startup ecosystems | Funding data, company valuations, market maps | Companies pivot, fail, or get acquired quickly. |

### Moderately Fast Domains (2-4 year half-life)

In these domains, information up to 3-4 years old is generally usable. Beyond
that, verify against more recent data.

| Domain | Examples | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Software development | Language popularity, framework maturity, best practices | Core concepts are stable but specific recommendations shift. |
| Digital marketing | Channel effectiveness, ad costs, platform features | Strategy shifts faster than tactics. |
| Consumer technology | Device sales, user behavior, app adoption | Hardware cycles are predictable; behavior shifts less so. |
| Healthcare (clinical) | Treatment efficacy, drug approvals, clinical guidelines | Check for updated guidelines and new approvals. |
| Employment / labor markets | Salary data, skills demand, remote work trends | Shift with economic cycles; verify against current data. |

### Slow-Moving Domains (5-10+ year half-life)

In these domains, information can remain relevant for many years, even decades.

| Domain | Examples | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Fundamental sciences | Physics principles, mathematical proofs, geological data | Core findings are timeless. |
| History | Historical events, primary source documents | The sources themselves don't change. Interpretations do. |
| Classical economics | Supply/demand theory, economic history | Long-run trends are more stable than cyclical data. |
| Established regulations | Constitutional law, foundational statutes | Unless amended, the law is the law. |
| Demographics (broad) | Population age distribution, urbanization trends | Large-scale trends change slowly. |

---

### Practical Rules of Thumb

1. **Always check the date** of the data, not just the publication. A 2024
   article citing 2019 survey data has 2019 currency for that specific claim.

2. **Distinguish between "the underlying reality changed" and "we have better
   data now."** Sometimes old data is still accurate — we just have more recent
   measurements that confirm or refine it.

3. **For market sizing and financial data,** always use the most recent full
   fiscal year available. Quarterly data is better than annual when available.

4. **For methodology and frameworks,** recency matters less. A well-established
   framework from 2015 may still be the best available.

5. **When you must use older data,** explicitly state its age and explain why
   you believe it's still relevant, or note the uncertainty it introduces.

6. **Never silently use outdated data.** Always disclose the date to the reader
   so they can assess relevance for themselves.

## Quick Reference: Source Tier Decision Tree

```
Is this a primary source (original data/research)?
├── YES → Tier 1
│   └── Apply reliability rubric. Even primary sources can be low quality.
└── NO → Is this an expert analysis or synthesis of primary sources?
    ├── YES → Tier 2
    │   └── Apply reliability rubric. Check for conflicts of interest.
    └── NO → Tier 3
        └── Use only for background, leads, or sentiment. Verify claims
            against Tier 1 or 2 before including in conclusions.
```

---

*Apply these criteria consistently, but not rigidly. The goal is to make your
evaluation process explicit and defensible, not to create a bureaucratic
checkbox exercise. When in doubt, be transparent about your uncertainty rather
than overconfident in your source selection.*
